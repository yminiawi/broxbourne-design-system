Guidelines

Images

Photography and illustration can help to make our communications more powerful and engaging.

We recommend using photography as the main image style throughout the Broxbourne website.

We are Broxbourne

We put our brand foundations at the core of what we communicate through our photography and illustration. We suggest using images that emphasise the approachable and supportive role of the Council, showing staff in action working with our citizens as well as capturing the people of Broxbourne in their daily lives.

Photography guidelines

Our photographs aim to capture humanity, whether using people, places or objects.

Photos should:

  • show a service being delivered
  • capture an interaction between people
  • feel natural and unstaged, capturing a moment in time
  • feel relevant and genuinely add value
  • be shot on location
  • be in landscape format

Consider, across the site:

  • showing diversity (young, old, different ethnic backgrounds)

Accessibility

WCAG 2.1

Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) are developed through the W3C process in cooperation with individuals and organisations around the world, with a goal of providing a single shared standard for web content accessibility that meets the needs of individuals, organisations, and governments internationally.

We aim to meet Level AA of the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) 2.1.

Accessibility testing

The digital touchpoints should be tested by disabled people, older people, and people who use assistive technologies. Sopra Steria will aim to do this as the service is developed.

Assistive technologies

The site should be usable by recent versions of screen readers and magnifiers, for example:

  • Jaws, NVDA
  • VoiceOver
  • Windows Eyes and Supernova
  • ZoomText
  • MAGic

The site should also be usable by speech recognition software including Dragon Naturally Speaking, and native operating system speech packages.

WCAG 2.1 at a glance

The site should be usable by recent versions of screen readers and magnifiers, for example:

  • Perceivable
  • Provide text alternatives for non-text content
  • Provide captions and other alternatives for multimedia
  • Create content that can be presented in different ways, including by assistive technologies, without losing meaning
  • Make it easier for users to see and hear content
  • Operable
  • Make all functionality available from a keyboard
  • Give users enough time to read and use content
  • Do not use content that causes seizures
  • Help users navigate and find content
  • Understandable
  • Make text readable and understandable
  • Make content appear and operate in predictable ways
  • Help users avoid and correct mistakes
  • Robust
  • Maximize compatibility with current and future user tools

Futher information can be found on https://www.w3.org/TR/WCAG21/

Writing guidelines

Introduction

The premise behind these writing guidelines is that customers do not come to the website to read about the Council. Instead, they come to transact when they have a specific need. In other words the website should be seen as the delivery channel for digital services rather than a catch-all for Council information.

Therefore presenting useful and usable content that enables customers to efficiently achieve their goals is the content writer's, priority, your priority - by avoiding overly detailed information or information for which the Council is not responsible or does not directly help the customer to complete the task.

This priority and these should be reviewed and if necessary updated every 12 months.

Last updated: 18.03.2019

Choosing content

Before you start writing make sure you have considered the following:

  • Is the content available elsewhere? If so, provide a link to it.
  • Is the content relevant to the page and the title? If not, it should not be on that page.
  • Does the content fully answer the question the customer is asking? For example: if the customer wants to know what to do, and the answer is to call a telephone number, make sure that the content covers what to do if the office is closed and the call cannot be answered.
  • Do you know why the customer has come to that page and what their mood is likely to be given the page’s purpose? For example: do they need reassurance, are they vulnerable, what should they be expected to do next and so on.

Attachments

Information that a customer needs to complete a task must be presented as content on the web page. Attachments must only be used if it is not practical to do so, or there is a statutory requirement that attachments must be in a PDF format. In addition:

  • attachments must not duplicate information available elsewhere on the website
  • attachment content must be published in readable, web friendly format so that pages are presented in the correct order when viewed in a browser
  • attachments must be regularly reviewed and tested for broken links (and accessibility where possible)

Page components

At the top of the page there should be a short, unambiguous, search-friendly page summary that explains the purpose of the page and what the customer needs to do on the page. In addition:

  • key information should be at the top of the page
  • use titles and subtitles as described below
  • sentences should contain only once concept or instruction
  • use bullets for lists or options or to draw the eye to specific information - wherever possible no more than five bullet points – absolute maximum should be seven (see below)
  • use a table for very complex information
  • avoid paragraphs of text wherever possible

Titles and subtitles

Use titles fulsomely so that the customer knows what the content will cover and, where appropriate, to direct the customer.

Do:

  • use sentence case only
  • use active voice and put the verb at the beginning of the title
  • pay attention to key words and make the titles search-friendly
  • use terms that are familiar to the customer
  • use subheadings to provide added clarity

Do not:

  • use gerunds (‘ing’ verbs)
  • SHOUT in the title (uppercase)
  • use jargon and council-specific words or acronyms

Addressing the customer (reader)

In all cases, when addressing the customer:

  • the tone must be formal and respectful
  • the personal pronoun must be ‘the Council’ (singular) with a capital 'C'
  • the customers must be referred to in the second person (you)
  • ‘please’ must be used whenever a customer is being asked to complete a task
  • when a customer has an option the phrase ‘you can’ must be used consistently (conversely, ‘you cannot’ will be used consistently where appropriate)
  • when an action is mandatory use 'you must...'

Types of customer

There are many different scenarios in which a customer may wish to interact with the website. Customers may be:

  • businesses or organisations; residents or visitors
  • informed or seeking information
  • familiar with navigating a website or unused to the internet
  • in a calm and safe place or in a temporary or volatile environment
  • seeking reassurance or help
  • using hand-held devices
  • non-native English speakers

Readability

In order to write content that is clear, plain, accessible, capable of translation and localisation, and will work with assistive technology:

  • do use active voice
  • do use British English standard spelling, -ise not -ize, programme not program (unless referring to a computer program)
  • do use double quotes for reported speech and when referring to an excerpt from a quote. Single quotes are only used as display quotes
  • do not use non-English terms – for example, ‘via’ must be ‘by way of’ or ‘through’
  • do not use non-English abbreviations such as: e.g, n.b, etc
  • do not contract words such as: can’t, won’t, shouldn’t
  • do not use jargon of any sort
  • do not use words that date such as: ‘new’
  • do not underline text – as this could be confused with a hyperlink

In all cases follow Plain English guidelines - http://www.plainenglish.co.uk. And above all, be consistent in terms of words, phrases and punctuation throughout.

Bullet points

Use bullet points to:

  • break up chunks of text or complex sentences (containing more than one clause)
  • clarify text
  • draw the customer’s eye to important information

Bullet points should start with initial lower-case, no full-stop (including for final point) and have no double-line spacing between them. They should be prioritised and where possible should not run to more than one line.

Lexicon

Avoid the word ‘Homeless’, use instead ‘in need of Housing’.

Drawing attention to the web

Don’t. Try not to use:

  • ‘click here’
  • ‘follow this link’
  • ‘this website’

If you do have to refer to the internet use: website, email, internet, intranet - one word, lower case.

Addresses

  • write street numbers, names and postcodes
  • always write out Street (not St), Road (not Rd) and so on
  • when letters are included, always lower case

Numbers

  • one to nine written out, 10 upwards in numbers with the following exceptions:
    • century always write out - for example, nineteenth century
    • bus numbers - for example, bus 5, 6, 7 or 27
    • times - for example, 6pm, 9pm, 5.30pm
    • where it would cause inconsistencies - for example, children aged 4-10 rather than four-10
    • where unit of measurement is abbreviated - for example, 4km
    • where version of software - for example, internet explorer 5
    • 60s not 1960s, and not 60's
  • always use figures with decimal points and percentages
  • use symbols with figures - for example, £243.57, 16%
  • spell out amounts over one hundred thousand - for example, three million, 12 billion. (The rule of figures over 10 still applies). Except with symbols - for example, £130,000
  • try to avoid numbers at the beginning of sentences, if this is not possible always write it in full

Currency

  • prices should be written with currency first then number, closed up, for example: £9.99, £10,000
  • do not include decimal point if whole number, for example: £5
  • millions should be written, for example: £1 million, £1.5 million

Dates

  • dates should follow the format: day, date, month, year
  • year spans should be in the format: 2019/20
  • only use the year if the event is not in the current year, for example: Monday 7 September 2000
  • do not use date suffixes, such as ‘st’, ‘th’ and so on

Times

  • use am or pm, no space, for example: 9am, 4.30pm, 7pm
  • use a full stop to separate hours and minutes, for example: 5.30pm

Distances and measurements

  • conversions (in brackets) to imperial units should be provided wherever this seems useful. Metric followed by imperial in brackets, for example: 16km (10 miles)
  • write out kilometres, metres and so on when writing out the number (one to nine), for example: eight kilometres (five miles); otherwise numerals and abbreviation (mm, cm, m, km, ft)
  • always write out miles, inches, hectares and acres
  • for area use sq ft, sq metres, sq miles, sq kilometres

Abbreviations

  • do not abbreviate days, months and so on
  • do not use full stops in abbreviations, or spaces between initials: BBC, US, mph, eg, No 10, PJ O'Rourke, WH Smith, and so on
  • spell out less well known abbreviations on first reference with abbreviation following in brackets. It is not necessary to spell out well known ones, such as EU, UN, US, BBC, CD
  • acronyms take initial caps: Aids, Isa, Mori, Unison
  • use all caps only if the abbreviation is pronounced as the individual letters

And or &

  • only use the ampersand where it is part of an official title or name, for example: Marks & Spencer, P&O
  • do not use the ampersand for internal departments, such as housing and social services (not housing & social services)
